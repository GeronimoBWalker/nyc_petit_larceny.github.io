In New York City, petit larceny includes minor theft offenses such as shoplifting and stealing unattended items. The plot above shows the yearly total of petit larceny incidents with the 2023 totals from each borough along the right side.

This project explores trends in petit larceny across the city’s five boroughs—Manhattan, Brooklyn, Queens, the Bronx, and Staten Island—from 2010 to 2023. Using visualizations, it compares larceny rates alongside a handful of economic indicators from census data to highlight patterns and disparities.

All data used in this project consists of yearly totals.

NYC MAP

Manhattan and the Bronx are closely connected, as are Queens and Brooklyn, while Staten Island sits more isolated. These geographic relationships help frame the borough-level trends we’ll explore.

Yearly Household Income by Borough

By normalizing the data within each borough, we can see the percentage of households in each of the five income groups from 2010 to 2023.

In all five boroughs, the upper and middle classes have steadily expanded over time, with the most dramatic upper-class growth in Manhattan. As of 2023, 69% of households in the Bronx fall into poor, poverty, and extreme poverty categories — a significantly higher proportion than in any other borough, highlighting a persistent economic disparity.

In 2023, Manhattan and the Bronx were the only boroughs with unemployment rates above the citywide average—highlighting ongoing labor market challenges in both areas.

Now that we’ve explored some of NYC’s economic context, let’s take another look at how petit larceny incidents are distributed across the boroughs.

Petit Larceny Incident Distribution

These boxplots show the yearly distribution of total petit larceny incidents in each borough from 2010 to 2023.

Looking at the shape of each distribution, Manhattan has been the most prone to spikes in incidents, while Staten Island shows a much narrower range. All five boroughs, however, experienced a post-pandemic surge, reaching new highs in 2022 and 2023.

Now, let’s adjust for population size to better understand where petit larceny incidents are more or less concentrated.

When we look at incidents per capita, the distribution shifts—placing the Bronx just behind Manhattan, and bringing Queens much closer to the smaller population of Staten Island. Interestingly, the two boroughs with the highest per capita incident rates also had the highest unemployment rate in 2023.

Next, we’ll explore broader trends in census-based economic data. By applying min-max scaling within each borough, we can visually examine how these metrics have changed over time.

Keep in mind the data represents yearly total number of incidents or the yearly total number of people in each economic category.

Petit Larceny Incidents

After applying min-max normalization, larger areas represent years with higher totals, while smaller areas reflect lower values. This makes it easier to spot relative highs and lows within each borough over time.

Manhattan, Brooklyn, Queens, and the Bronx saw their highest incident counts in 2022, while Staten Island peaked in 2023. Staten Island also showed elevated totals in 2014 and 2013, with levels in 2014 nearly matching those of 2022. Additionally, the Bronx experienced a noticeable rise in 2019.

Unemployed

Unemployment steadily declined across NYC from 2010, hitting a citywide low in 2019. However, by late 2020 rates began to surge—peaking in 2021 at levels not seen since 2011. A decade of progress was quickly erased, though conditions began to stabilize by 2022. A year before petit larceny incidents peaked, the number of unemployed people also peaked.

Armed Forces

The number of people working in the Armed Forces steadily declined from 2010 until a citywide spike in 2022, bringing levels back in line with those seen a decade earlier. A notable spike in petit larceny incidents occurred at the same time, mirroring the rise in Armed Forces employment.

Information

The information sector remained relatively stable from 2012 to 2021. In 2021, Manhattan hit a timeline low, while numbers also declined in Queens and the Bronx. By 2022, the sector peaked citywide—yet dropped to new lows in the Bronx and Staten Island the following year. This citywide expansion coincided with the spike in petit larceny incidents.

Wholesale Trade

Wholesale Trade employment declined in 2021, with Staten Island hitting an all-time low. While the other four boroughs continued to decline, Staten Island saw a notable rebound in 2022. This decline in wholesale trade jobs coincided with the spike in petit larceny incidents.

Manufacturing

Manufacturing jobs steadily declined across NYC starting in 2012. By 2021, Staten Island maintained relatively higher employment, while the other boroughs reached lows—a trend that continued until an expansion in 2023. These lows in manufacturing employment coincided with peaks in petit larceny incidents.

Natural Resources, Construction, Maintenance

Employment in Natural Resources, Construction, and Maintenance remained relatively stable with minor fluctuations until a sharp decline in 2020. By 2021, all boroughs saw significant job losses, followed by only a modest rebound later years. Yet another instance of job loss aligning with peaks in petit larceny incidents.

Agriculture, Forestry, Fishing, Hunting, and Mining

The combined agriculture, forestry, fishing, hunting, and mining industries showed varying peaks across time and boroughs. Manhattan saw its highest levels in 2022, but overall, these occupations don’t exhibit a clear or consistent trend across the city.

Arts, Entertainment, Recreation, Accommodation, Food Services

From 2010 to 2014, arts, entertainment, recreation, accommodation, and food services steadily grew, stabilizing through 2020. By the end of 2020, the sector saw a sharp decline across all boroughs—except Staten Island, where levels remained steady. Recovery began after 2021, though the Bronx experienced the slowest rebound. These industries declined a year before petit larceny incidents peaked.

Worked From Home

From 2010 to 2020, working from home remained relatively uncommon across all boroughs. In 2021, it surged to a timeline high citywide, followed by a slight decline in 2022 and 2023—though rates remained elevated. Remote work peaked one year before petit larceny rates spiked.

As remote work surged, we’d expect fewer people commuting. Let’s explore how different modes of travel changed over time.

Walked

In 2021, the number of people walking to work hit its lowest levels in Manhattan and the Bronx. While most boroughs saw a steady rebound in foot traffic, Queens continued to decline, reaching its lowest point.

Public Transportation

After a decade of steady use, public transportation dropped sharply across all five boroughs in 2021, followed by a gradual rebound in the years that followed.

Drive Alone to Work

Solo driving to work spiked in 2017, declined through 2020, and hit a low in 2021 before stabilizing again in 2022.

Overall, commuting dropped sharply in 2021 and rebounded by 2022—coinciding with the spike in petit larceny incidents. The rise in theft may reflect more people returning to public life.

Conclusion

This exploratory analysis examined trends in petit larceny incidents across New York City’s five boroughs from 2010 to 2023, alongside key economic and labor indicators. The visualizations revealed potential connections between economic instability, employment shifts, commuting patterns, and spikes in theft. Notably, boroughs with higher unemployment and economic hardship—like the Bronx and Manhattan—tended to experience higher rates of petit larceny, especially in the years following the COVID-19 pandemic. These patterns suggest that broader socioeconomic conditions may play a meaningful role in shaping crime trends.

Next Steps

To deepen the analysis, future work could explore neighborhood-level data, incorporate variables like police activity or income inequality, and apply statistical or machine learning models to assess causality. Incorporating qualitative data or city planning insights could also help contextualize the trends observed. As new data becomes available, especially post-2023, this framework can continue to inform urban safety and policy discussions.
